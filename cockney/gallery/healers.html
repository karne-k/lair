Lady's Favour

I've always wanted to do a St. George and the Dragon picture but expressing 'friendship' not battle. The picture evolved enormously during the rough stages (the dragon became female for a start and St.George turned into a woman and then back into a man again!) The final result was much more melancholy than expected (all to the good, it was getting sickeningly cute at one point!)

You might notice that both man and angel have lost the use of an arm, and the dragoness is a cripple, she can't fly. Their injuries bind the three togeather. Also, note the church has images of both men and dragons, they were not always at war.

By the way, a lady's favour is a medieval custom by which a knight is given a 'favour' - normally a scarf or other piece of cloth, by his lady before a battle or a tournament. It has a number of possible meanings, I'll let you decide between them..
Healer's Tent

This was done as the sister painting to 'Favour', another world, another healer and another war. In this case I've used a wide range of different species (five in total - count them!) in particular the cat being, on whom I'd been doing some work (see Sleek.) I'll admit that some of the ideas for this picture were blantently nicked from Mercedes Lackey's unparalleled 'Black Gryphon' trilogy - the healer in the picture was based on Amberdrake.

I think this is the first 'smooth skinned' dragon I've painted. That's mainly because the texture of scales is much easier to paint than skin, mainly because skin (or very short, fine fur, which is what I normally imagine dragon's wearing,) is very subtle and in addition, my favoured medium - acrylic - is not the best for replicating skin or other smooth surfaces. Its extremely quick drying time means it's very hard to blend and much better suited to painting texture. That dragon's skin was truely horrible to paint and took ages!

'Triptych' - 1995
Stoneware and glaze - 18K
I've done a number of pieces of sculpture, in various mediums and I love clay best of all. Unfortunatly I don't have access to a kiln anymore - [growl] This piece blends three separate faces, a human, Annubis - the egyptian god and Tiamat - a Babylonian she-dragon.

'The Shaman' - 1996
Stoneware and glaze - 21K
This sculpture was based on 'The Cage' (see below) and took the best part of two months to complete. It's a blend of a life sized human head with that of a tiger, although the tiger part might be a mask, hence the title.

An exam final piece, this languished in a deputy headmaster's office for a year until I finally managed to rescue it!

'The Eagle' - 1996
Wicker and wood - 20K
Originally intended to be made of welded steel and based on an earlier design made of drift wood, this avian rapidly took a life of its own. Made for my exam exibition, it eventually ended up with a wing span approaching four meters... a bit of a nightmare, considering it was put together with a glue gun! I was very lucky it stayed together long enough to display.

'Horned is the Hunter' - 1995
Watercolour on paper - 36K
Wild Pan, master of the forest. An experiment, using watercolour paints in the same way that I normally use acrylics. The idea was to exploit the blending ability of the medium in a way I couldn't normally (similar drying times but acrylics dry solid!

'Hermit' - 1995
Crayon on paper (head) - 45K
Pencil on paper (head and torso) - 45K
An early design for a fantasy species - roughly based on Mercedes Lackey's hertasi - and a multitude of similar creatures down the years. I finally used the design for the apprentice in 'In the Healer's Tent'

'Black Wave' - 1997
Ink on paper - 37K
Based on a certain Japanese artist. Can you guess who?

'Contrasts' - 1994
Acrylic and multimedia on masonite - 28K
The dance of the deer - the final piece for my GCSE exam. Think prehistoric :-)
