      Taxol® is a highly oxygenated, natural dipterpenoid, derived from the bark of the Pacific yew tree (Taxus brevifoli). It has been shown to be clinically active against a wide range of solid tumours, including; breast, lung, head, neck and melanoma and is a commonly used drug in the treatment of ovarian and breast cancer.

      The toxic effects of the yew tree have been known for many centuries and in 1856, a crude extract called taxine was found to be responsible for most of the toxicity. During the hundred years, chemical structural work was carried out on taxine and other Taxus constituents by European and Japanese chemists.(1) However it was only in the 1960s, during a massive plant screening program initiated by the National Cancer Institute, that it’s anticancer activity was discovered. The active chemical, detectable in almost all parts of the yew tree, was distilled by Wani and Wall and is called Paclitaxel (marketed as Taxol®). It’s chemical structure (see above right) was published in 1971.

      A great deal of work was needed in order to isolate Paclitaxel, as it is only found in extremely low concentrations. However it’s anticancerous effects are spectacular. During FDA phase II trials in 1988 (where a 15 - 20% response rate is considered good), it was shown to have a 56% response rate against breast cancer and 22% against certain types of lung cancer.(2) With about 45,000 death a year from breast cancer and 100,000 from lung cancer in the US alone, such a powerful drug is extremely important.

      Taxol is particularly useful in the treatment of Cis-platin refractory (resistant) ovarian cancer and can increase patient survival by an average of 13 months. It was approved by the FDA for the treatment of such in 1992 and for the treatment of certain types of breast cancer, in 1994. Research continues into the potential benefits of Paclitaxel in the treatment of a variety of different tumors and also into a Taxol analogue - Docetaxel (Taxotere®).
